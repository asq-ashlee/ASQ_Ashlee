AIDB Reality Check — AI's New Acceleration Phase

The moment:
The headline from Google IO was a price cut — their Ultra plan dropped from $250 to $200 a month. But when you dug into it, the cut came with a shift to usage-based billing for token-heavy use cases. Microsoft quietly cancelled its Claude Code licenses the same week, partly citing cost. Anthropic had already made a similar move. Three platforms, one week, same direction.

NLW's point:
The subsidy era of AI is over. Flat-rate plans that subsidized the most active users made sense when agents didn't exist. Now that agents are consuming tokens at scale, the math doesn't work. As Hedgy Markets put it: token-based pricing is forcing every enterprise customer to confront the actual cost of running these models at scale. The number turns out to be far higher than the flat-rate experiments suggested.

## Card 3 — AAW-003
**Format:** Product Question
**Audience:** Founder/Operator
**Channel:** Team Slack, internal discussion, product review

---

AIDB Product Question — AI's New Acceleration Phase

The distinction:
Most people are reading Google's agent integration into Search as "AI features added to search." That's not what NLW heard. His read: Google Search just changed categories. It has always been a one-time retrieval tool — you search, you get results, you're done. With persistent agents, it becomes something you set and leave running. Google's own example: instead of searching for apartments today, you tell Google your criteria and it notifies you when something matches. The search doesn't end.

The question nobody is asking:
If search becomes a 24/7 persistent agent for your users' questions, what does that mean for the information products you're building that assumed people would come back and ask again?

Persistent search doesn't just change Google. It changes what recurring engagement means for anyone building on top of information.

## Card 4 — AAW-004
**Format:** Team Prompt
**Audience:** Company Champion, Thoughtful Professional
**Channel:** Slack, team meeting, internal newsletter

---

AIDB Team Prompt — AI's New Acceleration Phase

Key idea:
OpenAI solved an 80-year-old math problem this week — a conjecture posed by Paul Erdős in 1946 that had stumped mathematicians ever since. Here's what NLW found more interesting than the result itself: the model used was a general-purpose LLM with no special training for math. The prompt was not clever. It was just a clear, well-stated description of the problem. Fields medalist Tim Gowers called it the first clear example of AI solving a really well-known unsolved problem. OpenAI researcher Alexander Wei summarized it differently: "10 months ago, I was ecstatic that AI could win IMO gold. Today, that excitement feels quaint."

Why it matters:
The question for your team is no longer what specially trained AI can do. It is what a general model can do when given a well-stated problem. That is a different question — and your team probably hasn't asked it yet in the context of your own work.

Team question:
What is the Erdős problem in our domain — the thing we've assumed requires specialized expertise or a specialized tool — and what would happen if we gave a general model a clear, well-stated version of it?

AIDB Reality Check — AI's New Acceleration Phase

The moment:
The AI water use conversation has been getting louder. Data centers are a political flashpoint. Communities are pushing back. NLW is not dismissing those concerns — he says the conversation should be based on facts. Here are some facts that flew around this week.

NLW's point:
Ethan Mollick ran the numbers on what it cost to solve the Erdős math problem: between 3 and 31 liters of water. Less than three almonds' worth. The annual water use of data centers overall is less than a fifth of golf courses, about a tenth of almonds, and about a twentieth of lawns. That does not mean AI has no environmental impact. It means the water use argument specifically has been significantly overstated. The next time it comes up, you now have a comparison.
