# Shareability Rubric

A moment becomes a card if it scores high on all five criteria.
This is not a spreadsheet. It is a judgment tool — run it fast, trust your instincts, flag anything that fails more than one.

---

## The five criteria

### 1. Specificity
Would this make someone think of a specific person, team, or situation?

Generic insight = low score. "AI is changing work" fails this.
Specific reframe = high score. "Coinbase blamed AI; the real story was a 47% crypto revenue drop" passes this.

Ask: *Who does this make me think of immediately?*
If the answer is nobody, the moment isn't ready to be a card.

---

### 2. Novelty
Does it reframe the topic in a way that feels fresh?

The test is not "is this new information." The test is "does this change how someone thinks about something they already had an opinion on."

Ask: *Would someone who already follows AI news feel like they got something they couldn't have gotten elsewhere?*

---

### 3. Actionability
Could the recipient do, discuss, decide, or rethink something after reading it?

A card that lands and sits is not a card worth making. The goal is to give someone something they can bring somewhere — a meeting, a Slack thread, a decision, a conversation.

Ask: *What would someone do with this in the next 48 hours?*

---

### 4. Trust density
Is it grounded, nuanced, and not hypey?

This is the AIDB brand filter. NLW earns trust by not overclaiming. Cards that overclaim, sensationalize, or flatten nuance violate the brand even if the underlying insight is real.

Ask: *Would NLW be comfortable with how this represents his take?*
If not, rewrite or drop it.

---

### 5. Channel fit
Does it naturally belong in a specific channel?

A card without a home is a card that doesn't travel. Before packaging, name the channel: Slack, email, LinkedIn, a team meeting, a client deck. The format follows the channel.

Ask: *Where does this live? Who hits send, and where do they send it?*

---

## Scoring

No formal score needed for early experiments. Run each moment through the five questions. If it clears all five, build the card. If it fails two or more, drop it. If it fails one, decide whether you can fix it in the packaging.

The rubric is a filter, not a formula.
