## 3. AIDB Sharing Exercise

Episode: [Who Cares About Consumer AI](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/who-cares-about-consumer-ai/id1680633614?i=1000766507145)

**Why this episode:** It holds a tension most AI coverage flattens — consumer AI is clearly working by every adoption metric, but the economics still don't add up — and that gap is exactly what professionals are arguing about right now.

---

**Moment 1: AI as a convenient alibi**

The episode pushes back on the unanimous media framing of Coinbase's layoffs as AI-driven. Nathan's actual argument: Robinhood reported a 47% year-over-year drop in crypto trading revenue the week before. Coinbase is a crypto company in a down market. AI is a cleaner story than "our core market halved." Axios was the only outlet that questioned it.

- **Who shares it:** A strategist, analyst, comms person, or anyone who has watched a business story get flattened into an AI headline.
- **Who they share it with:** A team having a real conversation about what AI is and isn't actually doing to headcount — and whether to trust what they're reading.
- **Why:** It gives them language to push back on lazy narratives without dismissing AI entirely. It's skepticism in service of clarity, not cynicism.
- **How I'd package it:**

```
AIDB Reality Check — Who Cares About Consumer AI

The moment:
Coinbase announced layoffs. Every headline called it AI-driven.
What got buried: Robinhood reported a 47% drop in crypto
trading revenue the week before.
Coinbase is a crypto company in a down market.
AI is a better story than that.

NLW's point:
AI will absolutely change employment. But letting it become
a convenient alibi makes it harder to see what's actually happening.

Worth the 20 minutes:
https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/who-cares-about-consumer-ai/id1680633614?i=1000766507145
```

---

**Moment 2: Shopping agents might be solving the wrong problem**

The episode draws a sharp line between two types of shopping: getting something you already know you want, versus the browsing, discovery, and quest that is itself the point. Agents may be good at the first. The second is a different human behavior entirely — and offloading it may cost more than people think.

- **Who shares it:** An ecommerce strategist, product designer, retail founder, or UX lead.
- **Who they share it with:** A commerce team, a marketplace operator, anyone building or evaluating agentic shopping features.
- **Why:** It avoids the lazy "agents will shop for everyone" assumption and surfaces a more honest product-design question: which kind of shopping are you actually building for?
- **How I'd package it:**

```
AIDB Product Question — Who Cares About Consumer AI

The distinction:
Some shopping is purely task-based. Get the thing. Done.
But a lot of shopping is browsing — discovery, comparison,
preference formation. The quest is the point.

The question nobody is asking:
Are we building agents for the first type of shopping
and assuming they'll work for the second?

If agents remove the quest, they may also remove
part of what makes shopping satisfying.

https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/who-cares-about-consumer-ai/id1680633614?i=1000766507145
```

## 4. First 30 Days

In my first 30 days, I would start by studying how AIDB listeners already share: what they forward, what context they add, and where it lands. I'd want to understand what Company Champions are already doing and where the friction is — they're the highest-signal distribution path AIDB has. Then I'd run one small experiment on a recent episode — identify three to five high-signal moments, score them for specificity, novelty, and actionability, and package the strongest one as a Slack-ready team prompt or a short briefing card.

I'd test it with real people in real channels — not a survey, an actual send — and track what I can: clicks, forwards, and replies. Early on I'd also just ask: did you share this? A direct reply saying "I sent this to my team" or "we talked about this" is the qualitative signal I'm designing toward. That's when I know the idea traveled somewhere decisions happen.

The goal in 30 days isn't a system. It's one repeatable thing that works. I've designed the first experiment here: https://github.com/asq-ashlee/ASQ_Ashlee/tree/main/ASQ_Career_OS/05_APPLICATIONS/aidb-growth-engineer_application

## 5. Taste

I would avoid anything that makes AIDB feel like a generic AI hype account: fear hooks, "this changes everything" framing, shallow quote cards, engagement bait, or over-automated summaries that flatten NLW's actual judgment.

AIDB's value is the combination: rigorous, fast, and editorially honest. That's rare and hard to maintain. Growth tactics that trade reach for trust are the wrong trade — not because speed doesn't matter, but because the judgment layer is what makes the speed worth anything. I'd rather make one moment easier for the right person to send to the right team than turn every episode into a dozen content fragments that travel far but land nowhere.

Wrong audience acquired fast is worse than right audience grown slowly.

## 6. Non-Obvious Idea

Most podcast growth treats the episode as the share unit. I'd test the insight card as the share unit: a short, recipient-specific package built from one high-signal episode moment.

For example, a product team could share a discussion card from "Who Cares About Consumer AI" that reframes the agentic commerce question — not "will agents shop for us" but "which kind of shopping are we actually building for?" Each card carries the episode link, a key idea, and a useful question. No summary. No clip. Just the moment, packaged for the channel where it actually belongs.

The episode doesn't need to travel first. The idea does.
